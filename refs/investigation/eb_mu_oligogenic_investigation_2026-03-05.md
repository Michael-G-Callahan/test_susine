## 1) Sparse vs Oligogenic: SuSiE-2 S4 vs Current `test_susine`

## 1.1 SuSiE-2 S4 (what the paper actually says)

From `SuSiE_2.txt` S4:

- Sparse regime:
  - `n=1000`, `p=5000`,
  - `k in {1,2,3,4,5}`,
  - fixed per-SNP heritability `h2_snp = 0.03`,
  - `beta_j = 1` for causal SNPs before residual-variance calibration.
- Complex regime:
  - explicit sparse/oligogenic/polygenic partition,
  - total heritability fixed at `h2_total = 0.25`,
  - oligogenic effects sampled from a two-component Gaussian mixture, then scaled to target `h2_O`,
  - canonical scenario: `3 sparse (50%) + 5 oligogenic (35%) + 15 polygenic (15%)`.

Interpretation:

- S4 is fundamentally **heritability-budgeted** and **component-count templated**.
- In sparse mode, effects are close to equal magnitude by construction (pre-scaling constant `beta_j=1`).

## 1.2 Current `test_susine` sparse implementation

In `simulate_effect_sizes()`:

- choose `p_star` causal SNPs,
- draw causal effects `N(0, effect_sd)`,
- no direct heritability targeting at this step.

Phenotype is then generated by `simulate_phenotype()` with `y_noise`:

- `sigma2 = Var(X beta) * y_noise / (1 - y_noise)`,
- so implied total heritability is approximately `h2_total = 1 - y_noise`.

Implication:

- control knob is **noise fraction**, not `h2_snp`.
- causal magnitudes are heterogeneous by random draw.

## 1.3 Current `test_susine` oligogenic implementation

In `simulate_effect_sizes_oligogenic()`:

- sparse count: `k_s = p_star`
- oligogenic count: `k_o = ceil(p_star / 2)`
- polygenic count: `k_p = ceil(1.5 * p_star)`
- total nonzero effects: `k_total ~= 3 * p_star`

Tier draws:

- sparse: `N(0, effect_sd)`
- oligogenic: Bernoulli mixture of `N(0, 0.8*effect_sd)` and `N(0, 0.25*effect_sd)` (mix prob 0.35)
- polygenic: `N(0, 0.08*effect_sd)`

Then each tier is rescaled to fixed L2-energy fractions:

- default `sparse:oligogenic:polygenic = 0.6:0.3:0.1`.

This is not S4-equivalent:

- counts are driven by `p_star`, not fixed templates like `3/5/15`.
- budgeting is L2-energy-fraction based, not direct per-tier heritability calibration.
- residual variance still set from `y_noise`.

## 1.4 Does your current `p_star=20-30` look like SuSiE-2 "realistic"?

Short answer: only partially, and mostly in "many signals" spirit, not in S4 design fidelity.

- In your sparse mode, `p_star=20-30` gives many heterogeneous causal effects.
- In your oligogenic mode, `p_star=20` implies roughly `20 + 10 + 30 = 60` nonzero effects, far above S4 scenario A's total 23.

If the goal is to match S4 scenario A count scale (`~23` total nonzero):

- solve `p_star + ceil(p_star/2) + ceil(1.5*p_star) ~= 23`,
- this is closest around `p_star ~= 8` (gives `8+4+12=24`).

Conclusion:

- `p_star=1-5` is closer to S4 sparse count regime.
- `p_star=20-30` (or `30-50`) is more polygenic than S4 canonical "3+5+15".
- For exact S4 comparability, add a dedicated compatibility architecture mode.

## 2) Revisit EB (mu=0): Why it can look strong now

## 2.1 What your latest heatmap is showing

- `susine_eb - susine_vanilla`: `+0.0265` mean, `66.7%` positive cells.

So your "EB can outperform vanilla" observation is real in the grouped metric view.

## 2.2 But dataset-level head-to-head is more mixed

Using per-dataset run-level AUPRC (base, unfiltered):

EB vs fixed `sigma_0_2 = 0.2`:

- `susie`: mean delta `-0.0025`, positive on `40.2%` of datasets.
- `susine`: mean delta `+0.0063`, positive on `47.6%`.
- `ash`: mean delta `-0.0097`, positive on `35.3%`.

EB vs best fixed among `{0.1,0.2,0.4}`:

- `susie`: mean delta `-0.0034`
- `susine`: mean delta `+0.0032`
- `ash`: mean delta `-0.0122`

Also, best fixed sigma was most often `0.1` (not `0.2`) across all three families.

Interpretation:

- EB is competitive with fixed defaults, but not uniformly dominant.
- "EB good" depends on regime and which fixed baseline you choose.

## 2.3 Mechanistic explanation (typical / good / bad EB runs)

For `mu=0`, SER Bayes-factor structure is controlled by prior variance through:

- `tau_j^2 = sigma0^2 / s_j^2`,
- `kappa_j = tau_j^2 / (1 + tau_j^2)`.

Log-BF term (up to constants) is roughly:

- `-0.5 log(1 + tau_j^2) + 0.5 * kappa_j * z_j^2`.

So `sigma0^2` acts like a temperature/sharpness knob:

- too small: over-sharp, brittle, can miss moderate true signals.
- too large: too diffuse, weak ranking contrast, more false positives.

Typical EB run:

- converges to moderate active effects,
- pushes some `V_l` to near-zero (automatic relevance determination),
- lands near mid-range fixed behavior.

Good EB run:

- estimated variance aligns with local signal-to-noise geometry,
- sharp enough to separate true peaks, diffuse enough to avoid over-commitment,
- often behaves like tuned fixed (`~0.1` in your current phase A grids).

Bad EB run:

- early coordinate updates commit to suboptimal peaks;
- variance updates reinforce that basin ("feedback loop"),
- or EB collapses too hard / too soft relative to the local optimum,
- can underperform tuned fixed settings.

Single-matrix probe (`eb_probe_mu0_singlematrix_compare.csv`) gave explicit bad examples where EB lost heavily even when residual variance estimation looked numerically stable.

## 3) Why sigma EB can work while mu autoscaling fails

## 3.1 Core theory difference

Prior variance (`sigma0^2`) is learnable from marginal spread/tails in effect estimates; it impacts second-moment structure globally.

Prior-mean scale (`c` in `mu0 = c * a`) is much less identifiable in sparse loci:

- only a tiny causal subset carries directional signal,
- null SNPs dominate the objective,
- annotation error and LD leakage confound slope estimation.

In your current autoscale implementation, `c` is estimated by unconditional weighted regression of marginal effects on annotation across **all SNPs** (with `tau2` adjustment). In sparse settings this is diluted toward zero by construction.

Evidence from `mu_autoscale_identifiability_probe.csv`:

- across `p_star in {1,3,5,10,20}`, mean `c_hat` stayed near 0,
- while causal-only slopes remained materially positive.

So the estimator is not "buggy"; it is doing what the chosen objective implies under null-dominated sparse data.

## 3.2 Current implementation inventory

Implemented in `susine` package:

1. `prior_update_method = "var"`:
   - yes, active,
   - used by harness for EB variance baselines.
2. `prior_update_method = "mean"`:
   - yes, but estimates a **constant** prior mean per effect (`rep(mu, p)`),
   - not annotation-scale estimation.
3. `prior_update_method = "both"`:
   - yes, joint constant-mean + variance update,
   - still not `c * a` estimation.
4. `auto_scale_mu_0 = TRUE`:
   - yes (init-only global `c` and `tau2` fit),
   - not wired into current `test_susine` harness call path.
5. `auto_scale_sigma_0_2 = TRUE`:
   - yes (init-only global variance scale `k`),
   - also not wired into current harness path.

Not effectively active in current harness:

- `run_model.R` calls `susine::susine()` with:
  - fixed variance case: `prior_update_method = "none"`
  - EB case: `prior_update_method = "var"`
  - no `auto_scale_mu_0`, no `auto_scale_sigma_0_2`.

Potential dead/inconsistent interface:

- docs mention `greedy`/`scale` update modes in some signatures/comments,
- but active update code paths are effectively `none/var/mean/both` and harness only uses `none/var`.

## 3.3 Small direct probe: autoscale mu vs c-grid

`mu_autoscale_vs_cgrid_probe_small.csv` (reduced quick check) showed:

- autoscale `c_hat` near zero on average (`~0.0`),
- best c-grid often around `c=0.5`,
- autoscale gave little/no gain over baseline,
- best c-grid showed sizable gains over baseline and over autoscale.

This is consistent with your original concern: local data are weak for identifying directional scale robustly, while explicit grid search can still recover useful settings.

## 4) Answers to your three questions

1. Is SuSiE-2 "realistic oligogenic" closer to your `p*=20-30` than `1-5`?

- For exact S4 design: no.
- For "more complex than sparse" spirit: yes, higher `p*` moves that direction.
- For count matching to S4 scenario A (`3+5+15`): your current oligogenic formula is closer around `p*~8`.

2. How to explain typical/good/bad EB (mu=0) vs fixed?

- EB adjusts posterior sharpness via `sigma0^2` and can improve over fixed defaults in some regimes.
- It is not uniformly better; tuned fixed still wins in many datasets.
- Good/bad behavior is a basin + temperature interaction, not just one scalar quality.

3. If EB can work for sigma, why not for mu?

- sigma is second-moment scale; data contain broad information for it.
- mu scale needs directional identifiability on sparse causals; null-dominated objectives collapse toward 0 or unstable estimates.
- your current autoscale objective is especially vulnerable to this dilution.
